What's happening

The first generation of AI was about producing answers. The next generation is about defending them.

For three years the question was: can I get the model to give me a useful answer? Companies wired up ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot. The wins were obvious and the stakes were low.

Then the AI work moved from "draft an email" to "approve a loan." From "summarize this meeting" to "decide which patient gets the trial." From "explain this clause" to "flag this transaction as suspicious." The model started touching decisions that affect people's lives and money — and the people responsible for those decisions started getting asked a different question:

"Show me what the model said, why it said it, and prove no one can change the answer after the fact."

That question is being asked everywhere now. By bank examiners. By healthcare compliance officers. By EU regulators. By plaintiffs' lawyers. By the company's own auditors, six months after the decision was made. And the current AI stack has no good answer.

Why current tools fail this test

The AI you're using today wasn't built to be defensible.

Every existing tool was built to solve a different problem:

  • The generation tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot) produce answers. They don't produce evidence.
  • The agent frameworks (LangChain, LlamaIndex, AutoGen) compose calls. Verification is bolted on as an afterthought.
  • The observability platforms (LangSmith, Langfuse, Phoenix) record traces — but for engineers debugging, not for regulators auditing.
  • The faithfulness judges (Patronus, RAGAS, Vectara) score answers after the fact. They don't produce the trail of what was verified against what.

Each of these is good at what it was built for. None of them, on its own, gives the bank compliance officer or the EU AI Act auditor what they actually need: a single artifact, produced at decision time, that proves what the AI was told, what it concluded, and what evidence it used — that a third party can verify independently months later.

That artifact doesn't exist in today's stack. That's the gap Orc fills.

A new category

Orc is a verification runtime — the layer between "the model said something" and "ship it."

The job is small and specific. Every time the AI is about to produce a decision, Orc:

  • Pulls the relevant evidence from a corpus you control — your contracts, your policies, your filings.
  • Asks the model to verify the claim against that evidence — with a structured verdict, not a free-form paragraph.
  • Refuses to cite anything that doesn't exist. Hallucinated sources get dropped before the verdict reaches you.
  • Writes a versioned trace: the retrieval, the prompt, the response, the corpus version. Reproducible months later, byte for byte.
  • For decisions that touch the outside world: routes to an approval queue with named-human sign-off.

When a regulator asks "what did the AI do on this case?", you hand them a single tarball. They extract it, they re-run the decision against the frozen evidence snapshot, and they get the same answer. That's the artifact.

Orc isn't a model. It isn't a framework. It isn't a dashboard. It's the contract between an LLM and the person who has to defend the work product six months later.

What it looks like

One command. Every artifact.

# 1. Create a workspace and ingest evidence you own
$ orc workspace create credit-memos
$ orc ingest ./Q3-filings -w credit-memos

# 2. Verify a claim against the corpus
$ orc verify "FY2023 EBITDA margin was 18.4%" -w credit-memos

# → SUPPORTED  confidence=0.91
#   reasoning: Computed 1247/6783 = 18.4%, matches the claim.
#   evidence: filing-2023-Q4.pdf §3 · p.12
#   run_id: 01KS0QT1MSV3NZA8ZSJK6VKJ7H

# 3. Months later, hand a regulator a self-contained tarball
$ orc audit export -w credit-memos --include-evidence
# → audit-credit-memos-20260519.tar.gz
#   contains every decision, every retrieval, every prompt,
#   every approval — all hashed, all reproducible.

That's the whole loop. Workspace, ingest, verify, audit. Also available as a Claude Code / Codex MCP server for teams already in those workflows.

Does the verification actually work?

Yes. On the benchmark commercial faithfulness models compete on, Orc just beat the leader.

HaluBench is the public benchmark Patronus AI built to evaluate their Lynx-70B faithfulness classifier. On a 504-item stratified subsample of that benchmark:

Orc (this build)
F1 0.864
general-purpose Claude Sonnet, no fine-tuning
Lynx-70B (Patronus)
F1 0.850
dedicated 70B fine-tuned classifier

Orc lands above the dedicated fine-tuned model — with a general-purpose Claude call plus a small safe arithmetic evaluator that the model can invoke when a claim requires computation. The benchmark is fully reproducible (one command, ~$7 of API spend) and the per-source breakdown is public.

The point isn't the F1 number itself. It's that you can hit competitive verdict quality and produce the audit artifacts in the same call. The faithfulness judges produce a score. Orc produces a score plus the trail a regulator can verify.

Why now

The regulatory window just opened.

Three things changed in the last twelve months:

1. The EU AI Act is now in force. High-risk AI systems deployed in the EU must produce automatic event logs, transparency artifacts for deployers, and named-human oversight records. Articles 12, 13, 14. Penalties are real. Most enterprise AI teams don't yet have a stack that satisfies any of it.

2. US regulators are moving the same direction. NIST AI RMF 1.0 is final. Sector regulators — Fed, OCC, CFPB, FDA, EEOC — are publishing AI-specific guidance. The phrase "model risk management" used to apply only to credit scoring; it now applies to everything an LLM touches in regulated industries.

3. The AI stack matured past chatbots. Agents, tool use, MCP. Every framework treats verification as a downstream add-on. None of them produce the regulator handoff. There's an open issue on LangChain's GitHub from 2025 explicitly requesting EU-AI-Act-compliant logging. The gap is acknowledged industry-wide.

Q4 2025 forward, every CISO and chief risk officer is being asked the same question by their board. Orc is the shortest credible path to "we have an answer."
