IThe shift

The first generation of AI was about producing answers.
The next is about defending them.

For three years the question was: can I get the model to give me a useful answer? Companies wired up ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot. The wins were obvious and the stakes were low.

Then AI moved from "draft an email" to "approve a loan." From "summarize this meeting" to "decide which patient gets the trial." From "explain this clause" to "flag this transaction as suspicious." The model started touching decisions that affect people's lives and money — and the people responsible for those decisions started getting asked a different question:

Show me what the model said, why it said it, and prove no one can change the answer after the fact.

That question is being asked everywhere now. By bank examiners. By healthcare compliance officers. By EU regulators. By plaintiffs' lawyers. By the company's own auditors, six months after the decision was made. And the current AI stack has no good answer.

IIWhy current tools fail this test

The AI you're using today wasn't built to be defensible.

Every existing tool was built to solve a different problem:

  • The generation tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot) produce answers. They don't produce evidence.
  • The agent frameworks (LangChain, LlamaIndex, AutoGen) compose calls. Verification is bolted on as an afterthought.
  • The observability platforms (LangSmith, Langfuse, Phoenix) record traces — but for engineers debugging, not for regulators auditing.
  • The faithfulness judges (Patronus, RAGAS, Vectara) score answers after the fact. They don't produce the trail of what was verified against what.

Each of these is good at what it was built for. None of them, on its own, gives the bank compliance officer or the EU AI Act auditor what they actually need: a single artifact, produced at decision time, that proves what the AI was told, what it concluded, and what evidence it used — that a third party can verify independently months later.

Documented LangChain GitHub Issue #35357 (2025) explicitly requests EU AI Act Article 12-compliant logging. MCP's 2026 roadmap names enterprise audit as a known gap. This isn't speculation — the gap is acknowledged industry-wide.

That artifact doesn't exist in today's stack. That's the gap Orc fills.

IVWhat it looks like

One command. Every artifact.

# 1. Create a workspace and ingest evidence you own
$ orc workspace create credit-memos
$ orc ingest ./Q3-filings -w credit-memos

# 2. Verify a claim against the corpus
$ orc verify "FY2023 EBITDA margin was 18.4%" -w credit-memos

# → SUPPORTED  confidence=0.91
#   reasoning: Computed 1247/6783 = 18.4%, matches.
#   evidence:  filing-2023-Q4.pdf §3 · p.12
#   run_id:    01KS0QT1MSV3NZA8ZSJK6VKJ7H

# 3. Months later, hand a regulator a self-contained tarball
$ orc audit export -w credit-memos --include-evidence
# → audit-credit-memos-20260520.tar.gz
#   every decision · every retrieval · every prompt
#   every approval — all hashed, all reproducible.

# 4. The regulator opens the tarball on their own machine and replays it
$ tar xzf audit-credit-memos-20260520.tar.gz
$ ORC_HOME=$(pwd)/orc-replay-home orc replay 01KS0QT1MSV3NZA8ZSJK6VKJ7H
# → re-executes the exact decision against the frozen corpus snapshot
# → produces the same verdict, byte-for-byte reproducible
# → no access to the operator's infrastructure required

That's the whole loop. Workspace, ingest, verify, audit. Also exposed as a Claude Code / Codex MCP server for teams already in those workflows.

VIIWhy now

The preparation window is open.

The EU AI Act runs in stages. The honest read is "the preparation window is open" — not "deadline tomorrow." But teams that show up in mid-2027 with no audit trail in place will not be comfortable.

2 Feb 2025
Prohibited practices banned
First wave of the AI Act in force — bans on social-scoring, real-time public biometric ID, etc. AI literacy obligations begin.
2 Aug 2026
Broad applicability + GPAI obligations
The regulation becomes broadly applicable. GPAI-model obligations and the governance / enforcement infrastructure (national authorities, AI Office) activate.
2 Dec 2027
High-risk obligations land
Annex III high-risk categories — biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, migration / asylum / border control. This is the date most regulated AI deployers need to be ready for. Articles 12, 13, 14 (event logging, transparency to deployers, human oversight) are binding.
2 Aug 2028
Product-integrated high-risk
Per the simplification agreement, AI components built into regulated products (medical devices, vehicles, etc.) covered under sectoral law fall fully under the AI Act.
Parallel · US
NIST AI RMF + sector regulators
Fed, OCC, CFPB, FDA, EEOC publishing AI-specific guidance based on the framework. "Model risk management" now applies to everything an LLM touches in regulated industries.

Every CRO, GC, and head of regulated AI is being asked the same question by their board right now: "show me what the model said, why it said it, and prove no one can change the answer after the fact." Orc is the shortest credible path to "we have an answer."
