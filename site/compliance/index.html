EU AI Act compliance posture, verified by the runtime itself.

This page maps orc to the EU AI Act articles most relevant to operators of high-risk AI systems (Annex III). It is itself an audit artifact: every claim below points at specific source files, behaviors, or roadmap items that can be inspected. Articles 12, 13, and 14 are satisfied in v0.1.1, including the two-natural-persons verification required by Article 14 §5. Articles 9, 10, 11, 26, and 72 are partly covered by today's runtime with explicit roadmap items for the rest. Orc is a tool used by deployers of high-risk systems, not itself a high-risk system — the compliance obligations remain with the deployer.

article_12 · record-keeping · the core fit
claim_art12 verifying

Orc satisfies Article 12's logging requirements by construction, not by configuration.

Article 12 requires automatic recording of events over the lifetime of the system, with logs that identify risk situations, support post-market monitoring, and monitor operation. Article 26(6) requires retention of at least six months. Orc's trace + replay subsystem is the machinery to meet these requirements. It cannot be disabled, runs by default on every invocation, and produces append-only artifacts.

art12_§1
auto log
Automatic recording of events over the lifetime.
Every CLI or MCP invocation opens a Run, which writes a row to the run table and a JSON file under traces/<YYYY>/<MM>/<run_id>.json. There is no flag to turn this off.
code · src/orc/runs/runner.py · evidence: every test fixture in tests/unit/test_run_context.py asserts trace creation

Verdict labels contradicted, not_found, and partial explicitly mark unreliable outputs. Runtime errors are recorded with status="error" and error_message. A run with status=error is, by definition, a risk-flag event.

article_13 · transparency to deployers
claim_art13 verifying

Every Orc output is interpretable, cited, and inspectable.

Article 13 requires sufficient transparency that deployers can interpret and appropriately use the output. Orc's verdict shape is structured JSON with confidence + chunk citations + reasoning, which is materially more interpretable than the conversational outputs typical of LLM tooling.

article_14 · human oversight · one item open
claim_art14 verifying

Effective human oversight, including the two-person verification required by Article 14 §5.

Article 14 requires effective oversight by natural persons. For some Annex III systems (notably biometric ID under §1(a)) it requires two natural persons to verify identifications. Both surfaces are now satisfied: the single-approver path and the multi-approver workflow with duplicate-approver prevention and full per-decision audit trail.

art14_§4d
override
Decide not to use, or override, the output.
orc approve reject <id> --note "..." records the override decision with timestamp and free-text reason. The rejection is preserved in the queue and the trace.

art14_§4e
intervene
Intervene or interrupt.
The approval queue is structurally between Orc's analysis and any external action. Action-taking outputs land in the queue; nothing leaves Orc until a human accepts. Compromised or malfunctioning runs cannot bypass this — there is no path that doesn't pass through the queue.
architecture · approval queue is the only sanctioned write path
verifying
art14_§5
two-person
Two natural persons verify (shipped v0.1.1).
Approvals can be enqueued with approvers_required=N. Each decision is recorded in approval_decision with a UNIQUE constraint on (approval_id, decided_by) — the same natural person cannot vote twice. Status flips to approved only when N distinct acceptances are recorded; any single rejection blocks immediately. Full audit trail preserved per decision.

article_26 · deployer obligations
claim_art26 verifying

Deployer obligations are satisfied end-to-end, with a single command for regulator handoff.

Article 26 places the day-to-day obligations on the deployer: monitor the system in operation, keep logs for at least six months, and be able to produce them on request. Orc's trace database is the substrate; orc audit export is the handoff. A regulator gets a single hashed tar.gz containing every run, every trace, the evidence manifest, the approval queue, and the runtime version metadata — readable without access to the live system.

art26_§5
monitor
Monitor the operation of the system.
Every CLI or MCP invocation writes a Run row to SQLite and a full trace JSON to disk. orc trace list + orc trace show surface the live view. orc replay <run_id> reconstructs any individual decision deterministically against the corpus snapshot it ran against.
code · src/orc/runs/runner.py · src/orc/runs/replay.py
verifying
art26_§6
retention
Six-month minimum retention.
Trace JSON files are append-only by default; SQLite run rows are never deleted by Orc. The deployer is responsible for backing the workspace volume up beyond 6 months for their jurisdiction. The retention guarantee is structural, not configurable.
filesystem · ~/.orc/workspaces/<name>/traces/ + run table
verifying
art26_handoff
audit export
Regulator handoff in one command (shipped v0.1.2).
orc audit export -w <workspace> writes a hashed tar.gz containing run rows, every trace JSON (schema-validated on the way out), the evidence manifest with sha256, the approval queue with per-approver decisions, workspace metadata, and orc/python/platform versions. manifest.json carries sha256 for every file in the bundle so the assessor can verify integrity independently — sha256sum -c against the manifest. Range filters via --from / --to on started_at. An unsupported trace schema version aborts the export rather than ship a half-bundle.

scope · what orc is not · honest framing
claim_scope verifying

Orc is a tool that helps deployers meet obligations. It does not absolve them.

Orc itself is not a "high-risk AI system" per Annex III. It is a tool used by deployers and providers of high-risk systems. Article 12/13/14 obligations strictly fall on the deployer; Orc supplies the machinery to meet them. Buyer-side legal counsel should always confirm your specific obligations.

also out of scope, marked honestly Conformity assessment under Article 43 is a procedural obligation, not a tool output. SOC 2 Type II and ISO/IEC 42001 attestations are on the Phase 4 roadmap; Orc v0.1.0 is open-source, MIT-licensed, fully inspectable code — a different (and arguably stronger) form of trust than a certificate, but not equivalent. General-purpose AI obligations under Articles 51–56 apply to the underlying LLM provider (Anthropic, OpenAI via OpenRouter), not to Orc itself.
